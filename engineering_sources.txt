describing how the KBE app is structured or how to operate it — see README.md

was researched, organised, and encoded during the knowledge acquisition phase.

================================================================================
A. ISA ATMOSPHERE MODEL
================================================================================

Source:  ICAO Doc 7488-CD, "Manual of the ICAO Standard Atmosphere", 3rd ed., 1993.
         Also: ISO 2533:1975, "Standard Atmosphere".

File:    ISA_calculator.py

----------------------------------------------------------------------
A1.  Troposphere (0 ≤ h ≤ 11 000 m)
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  Temperature lapse rate: L = 6.5 K / km = 0.0065 K/m

  T(h)   = T0 - L * h                       [K]
  p(h)   = p0 * (T(h) / T0)^(g / (R * L))  [Pa]
  ρ(h)   = p(h) / (R * T(h))                [kg/m³]
  a(h)   = sqrt(γ * R * T(h))               [m/s]

  Constants:
    T0  = 288.15 K,  p0 = 101 325 Pa,  R = 287.05 J/(kg·K)
    γ   = 1.4,       g  = 9.80665 m/s²

----------------------------------------------------------------------
A2.  Lower Stratosphere (11 000 < h ≤ 20 000 m)
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  T(h)   = 216.65 K  (isothermal)
  p(h)   = p_11 * exp(-g * (h - 11000) / (R * T_11))
  ρ(h)   = p(h) / (R * T(h))
  a(h)   = sqrt(γ * R * T(h))  = 295.07 m/s (constant above tropopause)

----------------------------------------------------------------------
A3.  Density Altitude (DA)
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  Used for display only.
  DA = (T0 / L) * (1 - (p / p0)^(R * L / g))


================================================================================
B. ENGINE TYPE SELECTION
================================================================================

Source:  Roskam, J., "Airplane Design, Part I: Preliminary Sizing of Airplanes",
           DAR Corp., 2005, Vol. I §3.2 (engine type / altitude thresholds).
         Raymer, D.P., "Aircraft Design: A Conceptual Approach", 6th ed., 2018,
           §10.2 (propulsion selection), §13.3 (thrust lapse with altitude).

File:    Drone.py — Attribute: engine_type

Rules:
  Mach threshold for jet selection (altitude-graduated):
    h ≤ 9 000 m      → Jet if M ≥ 0.40
    9 000 < h ≤ 15 000 m → Jet if M ≥ 0.50
    h > 15 000 m     → Jet if M ≥ 0.60

  Rationale: at high altitude the jet thrust lapse factor T_alt/T_sl ≈ σ^0.75
  (Raymer §13.3) makes a jet selected at low Mach infeasible (would need
  extreme sea-level rated thrust).  Thresholds match operational examples:
    MQ-9 Reaper: Turboprop at h ≈ 7 600 m, M ≈ 0.35
    Global Hawk: Jet (turbofan) at h ≈ 18 000 m, M ≈ 0.60

  Below jet threshold:
    High Endurance objective → Turboprop
    h > 4 500 m             → Turboprop  (practical piston ceiling, Roskam Vol. I §3.2)
    Otherwise               → Piston


================================================================================
C. WEIGHT FRACTION / BREGUET SIZING
================================================================================

Source:  Raymer, D.P., "Aircraft Design: A Conceptual Approach", 6th ed., 2018,
           §3.5 (mission segment weight fractions), §3.6 (Breguet range / endurance).
         Roskam, J., "Airplane Design, Part I", DAR Corp., Vol. I, Ch. 3.

File:    mission.py — method: fuel_weight_sizing, _fuel_fracs_parametric

----------------------------------------------------------------------
C1.  Standard segment fractions (Raymer Table 3.2)
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  W_warmup_taxi / W0    ≈ 0.970  (start / taxi / warm-up)
  W_climb       / W0    ≈ 0.985  (climb / accelerate)
  W_descent     / W0    ≈ 0.990  (descent)
  W_land        / W0    ≈ 0.995  (landing / shutdown)

----------------------------------------------------------------------
C2.  Breguet range equation — Jet
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  W_cruise_end / W_cruise_start = exp( -R * TSFC / (V * (L/D)) )

  where:
    R    = cruise range per leg [m]
    TSFC = thrust-specific fuel consumption [1/s]
    V    = cruise speed [m/s]
    L/D  = cruise lift-to-drag ratio

  Inverted to find achievable range:
    R_achievable = -ln(w_cruise_frac) * V * (L/D) / TSFC

----------------------------------------------------------------------
C3.  Breguet endurance equation — Jet
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  W_loiter_end / W_loiter_start = exp( -t * TSFC / (L/D) )

  t = loiter time [s]

  Inverted:
    t_achievable = -ln(w_loiter_frac) * (L/D) / TSFC

----------------------------------------------------------------------
C4.  Breguet range equation — Propeller (Piston / Turboprop)
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  W_end / W_start = exp( -R * BSFC / (η_p * (L/D)) )

  where:
    BSFC  = brake-specific fuel consumption [kg/(W·s)] (converted from lb/(hp·h))
    η_p   = propeller efficiency (0.80 typical)
    R     = cruise range [m]

----------------------------------------------------------------------
C5.  Breguet endurance equation — Propeller
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  W_end / W_start = exp( -t * V * BSFC / (η_p * (L/D)) )

  Inverted:
    t_achievable = -ln(w_loiter_frac) * η_p * (L/D) / (V * BSFC)

----------------------------------------------------------------------
C6.  Reserve fuel
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  Reserve fraction = 0.06  (6% of mission fuel, Raymer §3.5 rule of thumb)
  Equivalent to ~45 min hold at loiter conditions.

----------------------------------------------------------------------
C7.  Total fuel weight fraction
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  Wf / W0 = 1 - ∏(Wi+1/Wi)  for all mission segments

  This is the ratio of fuel consumed to take-off weight.

----------------------------------------------------------------------
C8.  MTOW iteration
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  W0 = W_payload + W_fuel + W_empty
  W_fuel  = (Wf/W0) * W0
  W_empty = f(W0)   — see Section D

  Iterates W0 until convergence (tolerance 0.01 kg, max 500 iterations).

----------------------------------------------------------------------
C9.  Specific fuel consumption values
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  Piston    BSFC:  0.45 lb/(hp·h) at best power  (Raymer Table 3.3)
  Turboprop BSFC:  0.50 lb/(hp·h)                (Raymer Table 3.3)
  Jet       TSFC:  0.60 1/h at cruise             (Raymer Table 3.3)
            TSFC:  0.45 1/h at loiter (partial throttle)


================================================================================
D. EMPTY WEIGHT ESTIMATION
================================================================================

Source:  Raymer, D.P., "Aircraft Design: A Conceptual Approach", 6th ed., 2018,
           §3.5, Table 3.1 — empty weight fraction power law.
         Roskam, J., "Airplane Design, Part I", Vol. I, Table 3.5.

File:    mission.py — method: empty_weight_fraction

Formula:
  We / W0 = A * W0^C

  Raymer Table 3.1 UAV / jet trainer coefficients used as baseline:
    A ≈ 0.95  (structural efficiency factor — lower = lighter structure)
    C ≈ −0.06 (scale exponent — larger aircraft are relatively lighter)

  Mission-objective adjustment:
    "Low cost"       → A multiplied by 1.10  (heavier piston GA structure)
    "High Endurance" → A multiplied by 1.05  (turboprop structure penalty)
    "High Speed"     → A multiplied by 0.95  (refined jet construction)

  Class adjustment:
    "small"  → A × 1.05  (relatively heavier small-scale structures)
    "large"  → A × 0.95  (economy of scale in large airframes)


================================================================================
E. W/P – W/S CONSTRAINT DIAGRAM
================================================================================

Source:  Roskam, J., "Airplane Design, Part I", DAR Corp., 2005.
           Eq. 3.6,  3.8  (take-off constraint)
           Eq. 3.11       (landing constraint)
           Eq. 3.15, 3.18 (climb constraint — jet / prop)
           Eq. 3.19, 3.22 (climb rate constraint)
           Eq. 3.23, 3.25 (climb gradient constraint)
         Raymer, D.P., §5.3 (stall constraint), §5.4 (maneuver constraint).

File:    WP_WS_diagram.py

----------------------------------------------------------------------
E1.  Stall speed constraint (vertical line on W/S axis)
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  W/S ≤ ½ * ρ * V_stall² * CLmax

  Typical CLmax assumed from airfoil type:
    Piston / Turboprop: CLmax = 1.6
    Jet: CLmax = 1.4

----------------------------------------------------------------------
E2.  Cruise constraint
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  W/S ≤ ½ * ρ * V_cruise² * CLmax_cruise

  Also defines the cruise operating point on the constraint diagram.

----------------------------------------------------------------------
E3.  Landing constraint (Roskam Eq. 3.11)
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  W/S ≤ ½ * ρ * V_L² * CLmax_land / (1 + γ_approach)

  where V_L = 1.3 * V_stall_land and CLmax_land ≈ 2.0 (with flaps).

----------------------------------------------------------------------
E4.  Take-off constraint (Roskam Eq. 3.6 / 3.8)
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  Prop:  W/P ≤ (η_p * TOP * σ * CLmax_TO) / (W/S)
  Jet:   T/W ≥ (W/S) / (σ * g * TOP * CLmax_TO)

  where TOP = take-off parameter (Roskam Table 3.1 by mission class).

----------------------------------------------------------------------
E5.  Climb constraints (Roskam Eq. 3.15 / 3.18)
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  Prop: W/P = η_p / (RC / 33000 + (W/S)^0.5 / (19 * sqrt(ρ/ρ_sl) * CLmax^0.5))
  Jet:  T/W = RC / V + 1 / (L/D)

  where RC = required climb rate [ft/min or m/s].

----------------------------------------------------------------------
E6.  Climb gradient (Roskam Eq. 3.23 / 3.25)
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  T/W or W/P constraint derived from FAR 23 / 25 second-segment gradient
  requirements (all-engine; single-engine OEI not applicable to UAVs but
  used as a conservative bound).

----------------------------------------------------------------------
E7.  Maneuver constraint (Raymer §5.4)
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  T/W ≥ n * (W/S) / (q * π * AR * e) + q * CD0 / (W/S)

  where n = design load factor, e = Oswald efficiency, q = dynamic pressure.

----------------------------------------------------------------------
E8.  Design point selection
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  Wing loading = min(all vertical constraints: stall, cruise, landing)
  Thrust/power = most restrictive horizontal constraint at that W/S.


================================================================================
F. WING SIZING
================================================================================

Source:  Raymer, D.P., "Aircraft Design: A Conceptual Approach", 6th ed., 2018.
           §4.3 (taper ratio), §4.4 (Eq. 4.6 — jet AR), §4.2 (root chord limit),
           Fig. 4.10 (structural AR upper bound ≤ 16).
         Roskam, J., "Airplane Design, Part I", Vol. I, Table 3.5 — AR table.

File:    Drone.py — Attributes: _wing_ar_roskam, wing_aspect_ratio, wing_area

----------------------------------------------------------------------
F1.  Aspect ratio — empirical (used in Breguet sizing)
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  Jet:       AR = 4.737 * M_cruise^{-0.979}   (Raymer Eq. 4.6)
             Clamped to [6, 12] for physical realism.

  Turboprop: AR = 9.2   (Roskam Vol. I Table 3.5, MALE class)
  Piston:    AR = 7.6   (Roskam Vol. I Table 3.5, piston GA)

----------------------------------------------------------------------
F2.  Wing area from design point
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  W/S determined from the constraint diagram (Section E).
  S = MTOW / (W/S)    [m²]

----------------------------------------------------------------------
F3.  Root chord / fuselage length limit  (Raymer §4.2)
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  c_root = 2 * S / (b * (1 + λ))   where b = sqrt(AR * S)

  Constraint:  c_root ≤ 0.40 * L_fuselage_cylinder

  If violated, AR is increased to satisfy the constraint:
    AR_min = 4 * S / (c_root_max * (1 + λ))²

  AR is capped at 16 (Raymer Fig. 4.10 structural realism limit).

----------------------------------------------------------------------
F4.  Span / fuselage length ratio cap
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  Maximum span = 4.0 * L_fuselage  (Global Hawk ≈ 2.75×; generous HALE bound)
  AR_span_cap  = (4 * L_fus)² / S

  If AR_min > AR_span_cap, AR is reduced to AR_span_cap to prevent
  wing / horizontal-tail geometric overlap at extreme altitudes.

----------------------------------------------------------------------
F5.  Optimal loiter speed
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  For best endurance, fly at minimum power speed (prop) or min TSFC speed (jet).
  Implemented as: find V that maximises CL^(3/2) / CD  (prop endurance figure
  of merit) or CL / CD (jet endurance figure of merit) by golden-section search.

----------------------------------------------------------------------
F6.  Lift-to-drag ratio
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  CD = CD0 + CL² / (π * AR * e)   (parabolic drag polar, Raymer §12.6)

  e   = Oswald efficiency factor ≈ 0.80 (subsonic, tapered wing, Raymer §12.6)
  CD0 = parasite drag coefficient — calibrated per mission class:
    small:   CD0 ≈ 0.030
    medium:  CD0 ≈ 0.025
    large:   CD0 ≈ 0.020

  CL at cruise: CL = 2 * (W/S) * wf / (ρ * V²)   where wf = weight fraction


================================================================================
G. FUSELAGE SIZING
================================================================================

Source:  Roskam, J., "Airplane Design, Part I", DAR Corp., 2005.
           Vol. I, Table 3.4 (fuselage length regression).
           Vol. I, §4.2 (fuselage fineness ratio).

File:    Fuselage.py, Drone.py — _roskam_fuselage_length_estimate

----------------------------------------------------------------------
G1.  Fuselage length (Roskam Vol. I Table 3.4)
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  L = 0.23 * MTOW_lbs^0.50   [ft]   converted to metres

  This regression covers jet trainers and UAVs.  The tool takes
  max(L_roskam, L_payload_min) so the fuselage always fits the payload.

----------------------------------------------------------------------
G2.  Fuselage radius / fineness ratio
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  Fineness ratio l/d ≈ 9  (Roskam Vol. I §4.2 recommended range 6 – 12)
  r = L / (2 * fineness_ratio)   [m]

----------------------------------------------------------------------
G3.  Fuselage structural mass (Roskam Vol. I Table 8.1)
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  W_fus = 0.328 * MTOW^0.5 * L^0.25   [kg]

  This is the empty-weight component for the fuselage shell and frame.

----------------------------------------------------------------------
G4.  Minimum fuselage length from wing geometry
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  To prevent wing / tail geometric overlap, the fuselage must be at
  least 4.5 × c_root_wing longer than the wing root chord:

    L_min_from_wing = 4.5 * c_root_wing

  Derivation:
    Tail must attach aft of wing trailing edge (root):
      L > wing_attach_x + c_root + tail_clearance
    wing_attach_x = 0.40 * L  (Raymer §4.2 typical mid-fuselage wing)
    tail_clearance ≥ c_root (1 chord-length clearance)
    → L > 0.40 * L + 2 * c_root  →  0.60 * L > 2 * c_root  →  L > 3.33 * c_root
    Add 35% safety margin → 4.5 * c_root  (implemented in Aircraft.py)

  Source: Raymer §4.2, geometric constraint derivation.


================================================================================
H. TAIL SIZING
================================================================================

Source:  Raymer, D.P., "Aircraft Design: A Conceptual Approach", 6th ed., 2018,
           §4.3 (tail volume coefficients, Table 4.2).
         Roskam, J., "Airplane Design, Part II", DAR Corp., §5.2.

File:    Liftingsurface.py, Aircraft.py

----------------------------------------------------------------------
H1.  Tail arm
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  l_t = min(0.65 * b_wing, L_fus - x_wing_attach)

  where b_wing = full wing span.  This is the distance from the wing
  aerodynamic centre to the tail aerodynamic centre.

----------------------------------------------------------------------
H2.  Horizontal tail area (volume coefficient method)
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  V_H = S_HT * l_t / (S_wing * MAC_wing)   [—]

  Typical V_H for UAVs: 0.35 – 0.50  (Raymer Table 4.2)
  Target V_H = 0.40 used in this implementation.

  Solving:  S_HT = V_H * S_wing * MAC_wing / l_t

----------------------------------------------------------------------
H3.  Vertical tail area (volume coefficient method)
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  V_V = S_VT * l_t / (S_wing * b_wing)

  Typical V_V for UAVs: 0.02 – 0.05  (Raymer Table 4.2)
  Target V_V = 0.04.

  Solving:  S_VT = V_V * S_wing * b_wing / l_t


================================================================================
I. UAV CLASS INFERENCE
================================================================================

Source:  NATO STANAG 4671, "Unmanned Aerial Vehicles Airworthiness Requirements",
           Edition 1, 2009.
         Teal Group Corporation, "World UAV Forecast 2023".
         Watts, A. et al., "The Military Use of Unmanned Aerial Vehicles",
           RAND National Defence Research Institute, 2012.
         Raymer §3 (payload fraction ≈ 15% conservative for ISR UAVs).

File:    Payloadrules.py — functions: infer_uav_class, _class_from_*

Design philosophy: UAV class is determined by the MOST DEMANDING single
constraint.  Each constraint independently sets a class floor; the final class
is the maximum.

----------------------------------------------------------------------
I1.  Range floor
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  < 150 km     → small   (LOS systems, e.g. senseFly eBee X: 90 km, 1.6 kg)
  150 – 500 km → medium  (MALE, e.g. Bayraktar TB2: ~300 km radius, ~650 kg)
  > 500 km     → large   (BLOS SATCOM, e.g. MQ-9 Reaper: ~1 850 km, 4 760 kg)

  Source: NATO STANAG 4671; Teal Group 2023.

----------------------------------------------------------------------
I2.  Altitude floor
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  < 3 000 m   → small   (piston-viable, e.g. DJI Phantom class)
  3 – 8 000 m → medium  (turbocharged/turboprop, e.g. Hermes 450: 5 500 m, 550 kg)
  > 8 000 m   → large   (HALE, e.g. Global Hawk: 18 000 m, 14 628 kg)

  Source: FAR Part 103; Jane's All the World's Aircraft.

----------------------------------------------------------------------
I3.  Endurance floor
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  < 4 hr     → small
  4 – 10 hr  → medium  (MALE rule of thumb)
  > 10 hr    → large   (fuel fraction alone drives MTOW significantly)

  Source: Watts et al., RAND (2012).

----------------------------------------------------------------------
I4.  Payload mass floor (strongest discriminator)
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  Assuming ~15% payload fraction (Raymer §3, conservative for ISR UAVs):
    < 3 kg    → MTOW < ~20 kg   → small
    3 – 90 kg → MTOW 20–600 kg  → medium
    > 90 kg   → MTOW > 600 kg   → large

----------------------------------------------------------------------
I5.  Mission objective inference
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  cruise_speed > 120 m/s (M > ~0.36 SL) → "High Speed"
  mission_endurance > 6 hr              → "High Endurance"
  Otherwise                             → "Low cost"

  Source: Raymer §3 (propulsion / objective mapping).


================================================================================
J. STRUCTURAL MASS — LIFTING SURFACES & FUSELAGE
================================================================================

Source:  Roskam, J., "Airplane Design, Part V: Component Weight Estimation",
           DAR Corp., 2005, Table 5.1 / Table 8.1.

File:    Liftingsurface.py (wing / tail), Fuselage.py (fuselage)

----------------------------------------------------------------------
J1.  Wing structural mass (Roskam Part V Table 5.1)
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  W_wing = 0.0051 * (MTOW * n)^0.557 * S^0.649 * AR^0.5 * t/c^{-0.4}
           * (1 + λ)^0.1 * cos(Λ)^{-1.0}

  Simplified version used:
    W_wing ≈ 0.036 * S^0.758 * (AR / cos²Λ)^0.6 * q^0.006 * λ^0.04
             * (100 * t/c / cos Λ)^{-0.3} * (n * MTOW)^0.49  [lb] → [kg]

  where:
    S  = reference wing area [m²]
    AR = wing aspect ratio
    Λ  = leading-edge sweep angle [rad]
    λ  = taper ratio
    t/c = thickness-to-chord ratio (default 0.12)
    n  = ultimate load factor (1.5 × limit load factor)
    q  = dynamic pressure at cruise [lb/ft²]

----------------------------------------------------------------------
J2.  Tail structural mass (Roskam Part V Table 8.1)
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  Same formula form as wing, applied to the horizontal and vertical
  tail areas separately with their respective AR, sweep, and taper.

----------------------------------------------------------------------
J3.  Fuselage structural mass (Roskam Part V Table 8.1)
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  W_fus = 0.328 * MTOW^0.5 * L_fus^0.25  [kg]


================================================================================
K. AERODYNAMIC MODEL
================================================================================

Source:  Raymer, D.P., "Aircraft Design: A Conceptual Approach", 6th ed., 2018,
           §12.5 (parasite drag), §12.6 (drag polar / Oswald factor).
         Shevell, R.S., "Fundamentals of Flight", 2nd ed., 1989.

File:    mission.py — _wing_parameters, _lift_to_drag

----------------------------------------------------------------------
K1.  Parabolic drag polar
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  CD = CD0 + CL² / (π * AR * e)

  e  = Oswald efficiency factor ≈ 0.80 (Raymer §12.6, subsonic tapered wing)

----------------------------------------------------------------------
K2.  Parasite drag coefficient CD0
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  Component build-up by mission class (Raymer §12.5 approach):
    small:   CD0 ≈ 0.030  (simple structure, fixed gear, antenna drag)
    medium:  CD0 ≈ 0.025  (retractable gear, streamlined antennas)
    large:   CD0 ≈ 0.020  (refined laminar sections, reduced interference)

----------------------------------------------------------------------
K3.  Lift-to-drag ratio at cruise
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  CL_cruise = 2 * (W/S) * wf / (ρ * V_cruise²)
  CD_cruise = CD0 + CL_cruise² / (π * AR * e)
  L/D       = CL_cruise / CD_cruise

----------------------------------------------------------------------
K4.  Maximum L/D (for loiter sizing)
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  (L/D)_max = sqrt(π * AR * e / CD0) / 2   (analytic max of parabolic polar)
  Achieved at: CL_opt = sqrt(π * AR * e * CD0)

----------------------------------------------------------------------
K5.  Wing loading adjustment during Breguet loop
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  During cruise the aircraft burns fuel and becomes lighter.  The
  instantaneous weight fraction wf tracks this.  The effective W/S at
  any point is:
    (W/S)_inst = (W/S)_MTOW * wf


================================================================================
L. STABILITY
================================================================================

Source:  Raymer, D.P., "Aircraft Design: A Conceptual Approach", 6th ed., 2018,
           §16.2 (static margin), §16.4 (neutral point, tail volume).
         Roskam, J., "Airplane Design, Part II", DAR Corp., §5.2.

File:    Aircraft.py, Liftingsurface.py

----------------------------------------------------------------------
L1.  Static margin
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  SM = (x_NP - x_CG) / MAC   (positive = stable)

  Stability thresholds:
    SM > 5%  → Stable
    0 < SM ≤ 5% → Marginal
    SM ≤ 0% → Unstable

----------------------------------------------------------------------
L2.  Neutral point (simplified)
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  x_NP / MAC = x_ac_wing / MAC + (S_HT / S_wing) * (l_t / MAC)
               * (CLα_HT / CLα_wing)  * (1 - dε/dα)

  Approximated in code using the tail volume coefficient and lift-curve
  slope ratio (CLα_HT / CLα_wing ≈ 0.90; dε/dα ≈ 0.40 for typical tails).

----------------------------------------------------------------------
L3.  Centre of gravity
----------------------------------------------------------------------
  CG computed as mass-weighted sum of component CG positions:
    Fuselage CG:  at 45% fuselage length
    Wing CG:      at 40% of MAC from leading edge (quarter-chord ≈ 25%, add depth)
    Fuel CG:      at centroid of fuel tank (between payload bay and cylinder end)
    Payload CG:   at centre of payload bay
    Tail CG:      at 40% of respective tail MAC


================================================================================
M. PAYLOAD LIBRARY SPECIFICATIONS
================================================================================

Source:  Publicly available UAV payload specifications; manufacturer datasheets.
         Listed in:  Payload/payload_library.json

File:    Payload.py, Payloadrules.py

The payload library contains entries for the following categories:

  eo_ir        EO/IR camera / gimbal system
  radar        Synthetic aperture radar (SAR) or AESA radar
  lidar        LiDAR mapping sensor
  datalink     BLOS satellite or LOS data-link terminal
  comms        Communications relay radio
  weapon       Air-to-ground munition / hard-point assembly
  flight_computer  Autopilot / flight management computer
  battery      Avionics / payload power battery

Each entry provides:
  label         Human-readable name
  geometry_type "box" or "cylinder"
  length, width, height_box  OR  diameter, height_cyl   [m]
  density       [kg/m³]  (used to compute mass as volume × density)
  default_for   list of UAV classes for which this is the default variant

Mass is computed at runtime as:
  m = volume * density

The variant selection rule (Payloadrules.select_payload_variant) picks the
entry flagged as default_for the current UAV class.  If no class-specific
default exists, the first entry is used with a warning.
